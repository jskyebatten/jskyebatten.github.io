// Computing Education Researcher

Jasmine Batten, Ph. D.

Investigating how educational contexts shape student pathways in computing — with a focus on broadening participation and evidence-based pedagogical practices.

About Me

When I walked into my first university CS course, I was expecting the collaborative classroom that had made me fall in love with computing in high school–peer-led, hands-on, and yapping + brainstorming with my fellow peers. Instead I encountered quiet classrooms echoing with the booming voices of professors, mired in an unspoken culture of competition. I looked around and counted four other women. I almost switched to nursing.

I didn't leave — but I never stopped thinking about the students who did.

I ultimately stayed in CS because I stumbled across a community of researchers who were asking the same questions I couldn't stop asking (hi fellow alums of the LEARN-CS Lab + Dr. Ross!). With this newfound feeling of belonging, I realized I would not be able to do it alone–I would only be able to help change the culture in collaboration with a community of like-minded researchers. Here I am almost 10 years later continuing to study the dynamic nature of computing education. And boy is it ever-changing.

My Work

I study both the visible and invisible forces that push minoritized students out of computing–the policies and cultures that create barriers, often disguised as rigor. I'm particularly interested in the dynamics that get overlooked as just structural features of higher education: the implicit messages embedded in how professors teach, the classroom cultures that signal who belongs and who doesn't.

One question has driven all of my work:

How do we improve computing higher education so that it works for everyone?

I've learned this is a really complicated question that requires multiple lenses, methodologies, and contexts, and the background knowledge from several different fields of study like psychology, social science, and education. As a mixed-methods researcher, I move fluidly between micro and macro scales, from semester-long ethnographic observations inside programming classrooms to national-scale data analysis, to answer the broader question of how educational environments shape who stays and who leaves computing degree programs.

As AI reshapes what it means to learn and belong in computing, that question feels more urgent than ever. I'm not yet sure what the answers look like. But I'm convinced that the researchers who understand the human side of computing education are exactly who needs to be asking them.
